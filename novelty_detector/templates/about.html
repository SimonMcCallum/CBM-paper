How It Works

Overview

The PDF Novelty Detector identifies which parts of a document contain novel or unique content versus parts that are repetitive or common. It uses LLM-based semantic compression combined with FAISS vector similarity search to score each chunk of text on a novelty scale from 0 to 1.

Algorithm Details

1. Text Extraction & Chunking

PDF text is extracted using PyMuPDF and split into overlapping chunks of ~150 words with 20-word overlap. This ensures context is preserved at chunk boundaries.

2. LLM Semantic Compression

Each chunk is sent to an LLM with surrounding context. The LLM generates a concise "prompt" (2-3 sentences) that captures the chunk's essential meaning. This acts as a semantic compression — novel content produces distinctive prompts, while common content produces similar ones.

3. Embedding & FAISS Indexing

Generated prompts are encoded into high-dimensional vectors using Sentence-BERT. Vectors are L2-normalized so that inner product equals cosine similarity. A FAISS IndexFlatIP index enables fast similarity search.

4. Novelty Scoring

For each chunk, we find the k nearest neighbors and compute: novelty(i) = 1 - (1/k) * sum(cos(e_i, e_j)). Chunks with high similarity to others score low novelty; unique chunks score high.

Theoretical Foundation

This approach is grounded in information theory and Kolmogorov complexity. The key insight is that an LLM acts as a semantic compressor: when asked to summarize a text chunk, it produces output whose information content reflects the novelty of the input.

Novel content — ideas not well-represented in the LLM's training data — produces distinctive, specific prompts. Common or boilerplate content produces generic prompts that are similar to prompts from other chunks.

By embedding these prompts in a shared vector space (Sentence-BERT) and measuring distances (cosine similarity via FAISS), we can quantify how semantically unique each chunk is relative to the rest of the document.

For a full mathematical treatment, see the LaTeX paper in docs/novelty_detection_theory.tex.
